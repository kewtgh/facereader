We often tell ourselves a very simple story about revolution.

People suffer. Their anger grows. The government becomes more oppressive. Then, one day, everyone has finally had enough. They pour into the streets, and the dictator falls.

In this story, revolution works like boiling water.

Public discontent is the temperature. At 80 degrees, people endure. At 90, the country begins to stir. At 100, the water boils—and revolution begins.

The problem is that history is full of societies that had been “boiling” for years, even decades, while the regime remained firmly in place.

North Korea's problem is obviously not that its people have not suffered enough. It would be difficult to argue that Romanians were still content with their lives in the final years of Nicolae Ceaușescu. Myanmar's military has faced enormous popular opposition. Iran has experienced wave after wave of protest.

Yet the survival of a regime does not seem to have any simple relationship with how miserable people are, or even with how many of them hate the government.

A society can arrive at an absurd condition:

> Ninety percent of the population hates the ruler.
>
> The ruler remains securely in power.

**Why?**

Because a revolution is not an opinion poll.

Even if 90 percent of the population opposes a dictator, that does not mean 90 percent will enter the streets at the same time. For an ordinary person, the decisive question is often not how angry he is. It is whether he believes that, when he steps forward, other people will stand beside him.

Perhaps, then, we have been asking the wrong question.

The important question is not:

**How many people oppose the dictator?**

It is:

**How many people believe that others will resist with them?**

---

## Imagine a Country with Only A, B, and C

Let us shrink the country.

The entire society contains only three people:

A, B, and C.

All three despise the ruler.

A now faces a practical choice: resist or remain silent.

If resistance succeeds, he may gain his freedom. If it fails, he may lose his job, go to prison, or die.

Silence also has a price. He must continue living under a government he hates. He must endure the same poverty, humiliation, and oppression.

But at least he remains alive.

So A is not really asking:

> How much do I hate this government?

What he needs to know is:

> **If I step forward now, what are the chances that we succeed?**

If failure means a 500-dollar fine, A may be willing to take the risk.

If failure means ten years in prison—or death—the calculation changes completely.

However intolerable his life may already be, silence can remain a perfectly rational choice as long as he believes the probability of successful resistance is sufficiently low.

---

But how does A estimate that probability?

He is not going to sit at home studying public finances, modelling the organizational capacity of the secret police, and calculating the loyalty of every army division.

Ordinary people assess political risk by watching other people.

Has B come out?

Has C?

Are university students protesting?

Are workers on strike?

How many people are in the streets?

Is the army moving?

So A watches B.

B does nothing.

A thinks:

Apparently no one is going to move.

So A remains silent as well.

---

What A does not know is that B is making exactly the same calculation.

B sees A sitting at home and thinks:

If A is too afraid to move, why should I be the first one to get myself killed?

So B also remains silent.

---

C looks at A, then at B.

Both appear calm.

C reaches an entirely reasonable conclusion:

Perhaps I am the only one who is angry.

---

Something very strange has now happened.

A hates the government. B hates the government. C hates the government.

All three remain silent.

A sees two silent people. B sees two silent people. C sees the same.

**Each person mistakes everyone else's silence for acceptance.**

**Worse, his own silence becomes evidence that encourages the others to remain silent.**

From the outside, this society looks remarkably stable.

From the inside, almost everyone may be furious.

This is not because people are foolish. Quite the opposite.

**Each person may be making the most rational decision available to him.**

The problem is that when those individually rational decisions accumulate, they produce an outcome that none of them wants.

In the language of game theory, under a particular structure of payoffs, “everyone remains silent” can become a stable equilibrium. If no one else moves, any individual who changes strategy alone may bear a catastrophic cost.

This is the most counterintuitive feature of dictatorship:

**It does not always need the support of the majority. Sometimes it needs only to prevent its opponents from coordinating.**

## The Mystery Is Not Why 3.5 Percent Can Be Powerful

Political scientist Erica Chenoweth's research on mass nonviolent resistance produced the famous “3.5 percent rule.”

In the original dataset, no major nonviolent campaign that achieved the active participation of roughly 3.5 percent of the national population had failed. Chenoweth later emphasized repeatedly that this was not a law of physics, much less a revolutionary formula in which reaching 3.5 percent causes a government to collapse automatically.

I have always thought that many people approach this finding from the wrong direction.

The most surprising question is not:

How can 3.5 percent of a population become so powerful?

It is:

**How can a country contain tens of millions of people who hate the government, yet still fail to put even 3.5 percent of them in the streets at the same time?**

Imagine a country of 100 million people, 90 million of whom are dissatisfied.

Each of those 90 million has one very reasonable condition:

> I will join once I see one million people in the streets.

What happens?

Possibly, no one comes out at all.

Not because there are no opponents, but because the first million are missing.

And where are those first million supposed to come from?

The first person waits for the second.

The second waits for the hundredth.

The hundredth waits for the ten-thousandth.

The ten-thousandth waits for the millionth.

**Everyone is waiting for a signal strong enough to prove that this attempt might succeed.**

**But the signal itself can only be created by people who act before such a signal exists.**

**That is what makes resistance so difficult.**

---

Sociologist Mark Granovetter described a similar mechanism in his 1978 threshold model of collective behavior. Whether a person joins a collective action often depends on how many people have already joined. A tiny change in one person's threshold can therefore produce a completely different social outcome.

Imagine ten people.

The first has a threshold of zero. He is willing to act even when no one else has acted.

The second has a threshold of one. If he sees one person, he joins.

The third needs to see two people.

The fourth needs to see three.

The first person steps forward.

The second sees him and joins.

The third now sees two people and joins.

The fourth sees three and follows.

A chain of action forms. Eventually, all ten participate.

---

Now change only one small detail.

The second person no longer joins after seeing one participant. He needs to see two.

What happens?

The first person still appears.

Then no one follows.

The third person is also waiting for two.

Everyone continues to wait.

The first person stands alone, is arrested, and the movement ends.

The two societies may contain exactly the same number of opponents. Their level of anger may be almost identical. Yet one produces a revolution while the other produces nothing.

The difference is not necessarily that one society is braver.

It may simply be this:

**In one society, the chain connected. In the other, it broke.**

Revolution is therefore never only a question of how much anger exists.

The real question is:

**Can anger be converted into an unbroken chain of action?**

## What a Dictator Must Control Is Not Merely Speech

This is why the most sophisticated feature of a mature dictatorship may not be how many people it can kill.

Killing is effective, of course. But killing every opponent is prohibitively expensive.

If a country has 100 million people and 50 million are dissatisfied, the state cannot arrest all 50 million.

There is a cheaper and often more effective method.

**Make sure A does not know that B is also dissatisfied.**

Let B assume that C supports the government.

Let C believe he belongs to a tiny minority.

Then each of them will independently calculate the same answer:

Resistance is not worth it.

Economist Timur Kuran uses the term “preference falsification” to describe the gap between what people privately believe and what they publicly express. A person may privately despise the regime and still applaud in public. He curses the government at home and attends political study sessions at work. He knows the propaganda is false, yet raises his hand in approval during the meeting.

Kuran used this mechanism to explain the suddenness of the Eastern European revolutions of 1989. Genuine opposition had long been concealed beneath public compliance. As a result, the regime misread society—but so did ordinary citizens, dissidents, and potential protesters. They all misread one another.

Consider what happens when one person disguises his beliefs. The effect is limited.

What happens when ten million people do it at once?

The entire society becomes false.

A sees everyone applauding.

B sees everyone applauding.

C sees the same thing.

Each person knows that his own applause is fake. What he does not know is whether everyone else's applause is fake as well.

This is why dictatorships can become obsessed with a sentence, a group of university students, a small demonstration, a low-circulation newspaper, an independent labor union, or a civic organization with only a few hundred members.

---

If we compare their physical strength, the fear looks ridiculous.

Ten students cannot defeat an army.

A newspaper cannot occupy the presidential palace.

A few hundred workers cannot stop tanks.

But the dictator's real question may never be:

> Can these ten people overthrow me?

It may be:

> **Could these ten people cause another hundred thousand to discover that they are not alone?**

Censorship, then, does more than prevent an idea from spreading.

It performs another, more important function:

**It prevents people from confirming one another's existence.**

“I am not allowed to criticize the government” is one problem.

“I do not know that anyone else wants to criticize the government” is a different one.

The first restricts expression. The second changes the expected payoff of action.

Yesterday, A believed he was the only person in his company who was dissatisfied.

Today, over lunch, he discovers that all five people at the table are complaining.

Tomorrow, he learns that the factory next door has gone on strike.

The day after that, tens of thousands appear in the streets.

A does not need to become suddenly courageous. His political beliefs may not have changed at all. Only his estimate of the probability of success has changed.

Yesterday, resistance meant suicide.

Today, it may be a gamble.

Tomorrow, it may even become the safer choice.

**The dictator is therefore not most afraid of an angry person.**

**He is afraid of an angry person who suddenly discovers that the person beside him is angry too.**

## Why Did Ceaușescu Fall in 1989—and Not Earlier?

Romania in 1989 offers an unusually clear example of this mechanism.

<figure class="fr-figure"><img src="https://kewtgh.github.io/PicSunflowers/img/2026/罗马尼亚-人民游行.jpg" alt="Demonstrators during the Romanian Revolution" /></figure>

Looking backward, Ceaușescu's collapse seems almost inevitable.

The economy was in crisis. Basic goods were scarce. Political repression was severe. The secret police were feared. The cult of personality had become grotesque. During the 1980s, Romanians endured harsh rationing of food, electricity, heating, and other necessities.

It is easy, from the safe side of the historical outcome, to say:

The Romanian people had already reached the limit of what they could endure.

But the more interesting question is:

**Why December 1989?**

Why not 1988?

Why not 1985?

If poverty by itself creates revolution, people should have revolted the first time they went hungry.

If repression by itself creates revolution, the regime should have collapsed when the secret police became powerful.

Structural conditions can explain why a regime is brittle. They do not, by themselves, explain why a system that survived for decades suddenly disintegrates in a few days.

The immediate story began in Timișoara.

The Romanian authorities attempted to remove László Tőkés, an ethnic Hungarian Reformed pastor who had criticized the regime. The first people who gathered around him were parishioners and supporters trying to prevent his eviction. More residents joined. The crowd quickly moved from defending one pastor to openly opposing the regime. The events in Timișoara became the spark of the Romanian Revolution.

There is a detail here that I think matters enormously.

For an ordinary Romanian, the question “Should I step forward and overthrow Ceaușescu?” was terrifyingly large.

Who is organizing it?

No one knows.

Will the army fire?

No one knows.

Will anyone else participate?

Again, no one knows.

The probability of success is almost impossible to estimate.

---

But for the people around Tokes, the first question may have been much smaller:

> Should I help protect a pastor I know?

The problem had suddenly become concrete.

The person at the center of it was identifiable.

The first participants knew one another.

Some degree of trust and social connection already existed among them.

I am not standing alone before the entire machinery of the state. Members of my congregation are beside me.

**A national coordination problem had temporarily been reduced to a local action problem inside an existing social network.**

---

Histories of revolution often make a predictable mistake.

Because the government was eventually overthrown, we assume that the first people who came out intended to overthrow it from the beginning.

Not necessarily.

Large social transformations often do not begin with a grand objective.

They may begin with an attempt to protect one person.

A funeral.

A protest against a price increase.

A disputed election.

A refusal to accept an eviction.

The first participants may not be thinking about revolution at all.

But they do something essential:

They allow those who come later to see that **someone has already stepped forward**.

The first threshold is crossed.

Then the second.

Then the third.

The protests in Timișoara were violently repressed, but they did not disappear. Workers and more residents joined, and the movement expanded rapidly.

Romanian anger did not suddenly come into existence during those few days.

Only one thing was genuinely new:

**The anger had become visible.**

## A Televised Rally Meant to Prove That “The People Love Me”

Ceaușescu then made a decision that was politically disastrous from the perspective of coordination.

On December 21, 1989, he addressed a mass rally in Bucharest.

To us, this can look like nothing more than the vanity of a dictator.

A square.

Banners.

Applause.

The leader speaking to the nation.

But such events are not merely decorative. The spectacle itself performs a political function. It tells every viewer:

> The people still support the leader.

Many people in the square may know that the rally has been organized. That does not make the performance useless.

A may know that the event is staged, but A does not necessarily know that B also sees through it.

A may think:

I dislike Ceaușescu, but perhaps everyone else still supports him.

B may think the same.

So may C.

Tens of thousands of people can therefore stand in a square, many of them hostile to the government, while each individual still sees what appears to be a sea of applause.

The December 21 rally was designed as a familiar political ritual: carefully assembled crowds, a controlled performance, and a televised image of popular loyalty.

**But that day, the script broke.**

---

As Ceaușescu spoke, noise and protest emerged from the crowd.

The order of the event began to collapse. The live broadcast captured his momentary confusion.

Watch the footage, and the most revealing image may not be the crowd. It may be Ceaușescu's face.

<figure class="fr-figure"><img src="https://kewtgh.github.io/PicSunflowers/img/2026/齐奥塞斯库-倒台演讲.jpg" alt="Nicolae Ceaușescu during his final public speech" /></figure>

For a moment, he seems unable to understand what is happening.

Why?

Because the machine had worked for too long.

The leader speaks. The crowd applauds. Television broadcasts the event. The nation watches.

For decades, the ritual had repeated the same political message:

Everyone is obeying.

This time, someone failed to applaud on cue.

More importantly:

**Everyone could see it.**

That is what made the live broadcast so dangerous.

---

Before, I knew that I was dissatisfied.

Now, I know that others are dissatisfied too.

But there is another step.

I know that other people have seen this dissatisfaction.

They know that I have seen it.

This is very different from “everyone complains in private.”

In game theory, this approaches what is called **common knowledge**.

Consider a simple example.

A knows that a door is unlocked.

B also knows that the door is unlocked.

But A does not know whether B knows.

They may still fail to coordinate an escape.

What changes behavior is not merely that both possess the same information. It is that A knows B knows, B knows A knows, and each can begin to predict the other's action.

Ceaușescu's television rally was intended to manufacture one form of common knowledge:

> Everyone supports me.

Instead, it accidentally manufactured another:

> **Everyone has now seen that the regime is being openly defied.**

Did the political beliefs of the Romanian population change dramatically in that minute?

I doubt it.

The people who hated Ceaușescu the day before were largely the same people who hated him that day.

What changed was:

**Their estimate of everyone else.**

Once that variable changed, the old equilibrium of silence was no longer stable.

## But Even a Huge Crowd Cannot Defeat a Tank

If the argument ended here, it would be a romantic story.

People discover one another. They enter the streets. The dictator falls.

Beautiful.

But that is not how power works.

One hundred thousand people may occupy a square. The state still has guns, tanks, barracks, intelligence agencies, and prisons.

A modern state is not an ancient tyrant with a sword. A modern dictator does not have to kill anyone personally. He commands an organization built to act on his behalf.

---

So when a mature dictatorship collapses, there is usually more than one game taking place.

There is the game among citizens.

And there is another, often more decisive game:

**The game inside the ruling apparatus.**

A soldier also calculates.

Should I fire?

If the regime is likely to survive for another thirty years, refusing an order may mean prison, a military tribunal, or death.

Under those conditions, obedience is the safest strategy.

But what if Ceaușescu will be gone in three days?

What happens to the soldier who fires today?

Will he be prosecuted? Will he stand trial? Will the order he obeys today become evidence against him next week?

---

An officer is calculating too.

Will the neighboring unit carry out its orders?

Can my superior still control the situation?

Are the other generals loyal?

Will the security services fight to the end?

---

A minister makes the same kind of calculation.

What is the return on continued loyalty?

What is the risk of defecting?

This is why I am skeptical when revolutions are described as moments in which “the people suddenly awakened.”

Many people do not awaken at all.

An official who served the dictator yesterday may defect today.

Not because he read Rousseau overnight.

Not because he suddenly fell in love with democracy.

More likely:

**His best strategy changed.**

---

A dangerous feedback loop now begins.

More citizens enter the streets.

The military becomes less certain that the regime can restore control.

Its willingness to repress declines.

Citizens notice that soldiers are no longer reliably firing.

That encourages more people to come out.

The larger the crowd becomes, the more officers question whether the regime will survive.

This is not a linear process. It is a positive feedback loop.

On December 22, Ceaușescu fled Bucharest. By then, the coercive institutions of the state could no longer function as a stable, unified system capable of protecting him. The revolution—and the violence that followed—was chaotic. The roles of the army, the security services, and the new political leadership remain contested.

But from the standpoint of individual decision-making, the important change is clear.

Yesterday, the citizen asked:

> Should I face the army alone?

Today, he asks:

> Will the army definitely fire at me?

---

The soldier's question changes as well.

Yesterday:

> Should I obey the government?

Today:

> **Do I want to be the last man who kills for Ceaușescu?**

This is the most dangerous moment for a dictatorship.

During stable periods, betraying the dictator is extremely costly.

But once enough people believe that he may lose, the direction of risk can reverse.

**The most dangerous choice may no longer be abandoning the dictator. It may be remaining loyal to him after everyone else has left.**

That is how a regime that looked stable for decades can collapse in a matter of days.

Observers will say:

The people finally became angry.

But the people may have been angry for years.

What really changed was:

**Everyone began recalculating at once.**

## Why Do Revolutions Always Look So Sudden?

After a regime falls, we can always find reasons why it was destined to collapse.

The economy was failing.

Corruption was widespread.

The population was angry.

The ruler was aging.

The bureaucracy had become rigid.

All of these explanations may be correct.

But I keep returning to a simpler question:

**If the regime was already destined to fall, why had it not fallen yesterday?**

This is central to Timur Kuran's explanation of the Eastern European revolutions of 1989.

Public obedience concealed private opposition. The surface of society therefore revealed very little about the true level of discontent. But even perfect knowledge of how many people privately opposed the regime would not have allowed us to predict the exact timing of revolution, because each person's action still depended on what he expected everyone else to do.

---

A society can remain unchanged for a very long time.

A is silent.

B is silent.

C is silent too.

Then something happens—often something that does not initially look historic.

A steps forward.

B sees A, and his threshold is crossed.

B joins.

C sees A and B.

C follows.

Then D, E, and F.

Problems that accumulated for decades suddenly erupt within days.

---

Historians then look backward and say:

The revolution was inevitable.

Perhaps.

But on the day before it began, no one knew whether the chain of action would connect.

The first person may step forward, and the second may follow.

Or he may not.

An act of police brutality may trigger nationwide protest.

Or it may frighten everyone back into silence.

A soldier who refuses to fire may destabilize an entire army.

Or he may be executed the next morning, and nothing else may happen.

This is why revolutions have such a peculiar quality:

**Afterward, they look unavoidable. Beforehand, they are extraordinarily difficult to predict.**

Not because history has no structure, but because every participant in the system is observing every other participant.

A is judging B.

B is judging C.

An officer is judging other officers.

A minister is judging the dictator.

An event changes information.

Information changes behavior.

Behavior creates new information.

Then the equilibrium flips.

## How Important Are External Forces?

I once leaned toward a stronger conclusion:

**Under the conditions of the modern state, a mature dictatorship is almost impossible to overthrow without outside intervention.**

I now think that claim needs to be stated more precisely.

**External forces can be decisive, but they do not necessarily need to send an army into the capital.**

There was a crucial difference between Eastern Europe in 1989 and Hungary in 1956 or Czechoslovakia in 1968.

Under Mikhail Gorbachev, the Soviet Union had abandoned the Brezhnev Doctrine—the assumption that Moscow would use military force to preserve communist rule in Eastern Europe.

Soviet tanks did not enter Bucharest to help Romanians capture Ceaușescu.

But:

**The fact that Soviet tanks would not come to save Ceaușescu was itself an enormously important variable.**

---

A general may previously have believed:

Even if the situation inside the country deteriorates, an ally will intervene.

Now he knows it will not.

An ordinary citizen may previously have believed:

Communist governments simply do not fall.

Then Poland changes.

Hungary changes.

The Berlin Wall falls.

One regime after another begins to weaken.

Naturally, his estimate of whether Ceaușescu can be overthrown changes as well.

The sequence of political transformations across Eastern Europe, combined with the Soviet decision not to enforce communist rule militarily, altered expectations throughout the region.

---

This is why the importance of external power cannot always be measured by counting weapons.

Military defeat.

The withdrawal of an ally.

The loss of international aid.

A revolution in a neighboring country.

A foreign patron ending its support.

All of these can perform the same political function:

**They change what crucial domestic actors believe about who will ultimately win.**

So I would now frame the question differently.

Not:

Did foreigners help the revolution?

But:

**What changed the payoff calculations of citizens, soldiers, officials, and members of the ruling coalition?**

If nothing changes the expectations of those groups, then a mature dictatorship with a modern state apparatus remains extremely difficult to overthrow merely because “the people are suffering more and more.”

**Suffering does not organize itself. Anger does not coordinate itself.**

## What Dictatorship Really Depends On May Not Be Support

We usually understand dictatorship as a relationship of force.

The state has an army. The people do not. Therefore, the people obey.

That is true.

But force alone cannot explain something else:

**How can a regime with hundreds of thousands of armed personnel lose control in only a few days?**

Because the state's guns do not fire themselves.

There is a person behind each gun.

That person is also watching, fearing, and calculating.

A modern dictator cannot personally control tens of millions of people.

**What sustains dictatorship is an enormous social system remaining, all at once, inside a particular stable equilibrium.**

Citizens believe that other citizens will not act.

Bureaucrats believe that their colleagues will continue to obey.

Soldiers believe that other soldiers will carry out their orders.

Generals believe that the regime will survive.

Businesspeople believe that resistance is futile.

Journalists believe that censorship still works.

Each person chooses the strategy that appears safest based on what he expects everyone else to do.

---

So:

**No single person creates the dictatorship alone.**

**But the rational choices of millions of individuals can collectively sustain it.**

That, I think, is what makes modern dictatorship so frightening.

A regime does not need to make everyone love it.

It may not even need a majority to believe in it.

**It needs each opponent to believe that he is alone.**

People can suffer for ten years.

Twenty.

Thirty.

As long as they continue to believe that other people will not move, that soldiers will fire, that officials will obey, and that the regime will survive, silence remains rational.

---

Change begins when A suddenly discovers that B is also opposed.

B learns that C is already in the streets.

A soldier notices that the neighboring unit has refused to fire.

A general discovers that several other generals are waiting rather than acting.

An official realizes that the dictator may not survive the week.

On that day, no one's morality needs to change.

The public does not suddenly become braver.

The officers do not suddenly become just.

The people in the streets today may be exactly the same people who applauded yesterday.

Only one thing has changed:

**Their beliefs about what everyone else will do.**

And then the whole world changes with them.

We often ask:

> When will the people finally reach the point where they can endure no more?

I increasingly think that this question is wrong from the beginning.

The better question is:

> **When does a silent person suddenly discover that he is not alone?**

Because what a dictator finally loses is often not public support. That support may have disappeared years earlier.

What he truly loses is:

**The shared belief that he cannot fall.**

The strongest wall protecting a dictatorship may not be its prisons, secret police, or tanks.

It may also be the sentence inside every opponent's mind:

> **What difference can I make if I am the only one?**

A revolution may not truly begin when the first stone is thrown.

It may begin when enough people suddenly realize:

> **The others were waiting for me too.**
